 NOTE 1:  FLOW RIDE  —  SHIFT for 2× speed on flow tiles
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

What it does:
  When the player holds SHIFT on a tile that has a water current,
  the microbe (ARKE) "rides" the flow — moving in the direction
  of the current at DOUBLE the normal speed.

How it works in the code (player.gd, lines 108–127):
  1. The player presses and holds the SHIFT key.
  2. The game checks: "Does the tile I'm standing on have a flow?"
     → It calls  world_ref.get_flow(tile_x, tile_y)
     → This returns two things: a direction (N/S/E/W) and a speed (0.0–1.0).
  3. If the flow speed is greater than 0.05 (i.e. the current is
     strong enough to carry the microbe), the player moves one tile
     in the flow direction.
  4. The movement timer is cut in half:
       Normal movement = 0.4 seconds per tile
       Flow riding     = 0.2 seconds per tile  (2× faster)
     This is done by dividing by "flow_ride_mult" which equals 2.0
     (set in game_data.gd, line 53).
  5. Each flow-ride step costs 0.5 energy — so it is fast but not free.

Why it matters for the game:
  Flow tiles simulate real water currents in porous soil.
  The player can use them as "highways" to travel quickly across
  the map — but they must plan their route because the current
  only goes in ONE direction per tile. Riding against the current
  is not possible.

The science behind it:
  In real subsurface environments, dissolved microbes travel through
  porous media via advection — they are carried along by groundwater
  flow. CompLaB3D models this using a pressure-driven flow field
  computed from Darcy's Law. The game simplifies this into discrete
  flow directions per tile.


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
 NOTE 2:  DIVIDE  —  SPACE at GR=100 → biofilm colony
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

What it does:
  When the player's Growth (GR) bar reaches 100, they can press
  SPACE to divide — placing a permanent biofilm colony on the map.
  Growth then resets to 0, and you start filling the bar again.

How GR (Growth) fills up:
  Every time ARKE eats a substrate, growth increases. Different
  substrates give different amounts:
    CH4 (methane)    → +25 growth   (highest — the "jackpot" food)
    NO3 (nitrate)    → +18 growth
    O2  (oxygen)     → +15 growth
    MN4 (manganese)  → +12 growth
    FE3 (iron)       → +10 growth
    SO4 (sulfate)    → +8  growth   (lowest)
  Growth is capped at 100. Once it hits 100 the HUD flashes
  "SPACE!" in yellow to tell the player they can divide.

How division works in the code (player.gd, lines 202–233):
  1. Player presses SPACE → game calls  try_divide()
  2. The game looks for an open pore tile next to the player.
     If no neighbors are open, it searches 2 tiles away.
  3. Among all open spots, it picks the one FURTHEST from solid
     walls (using a distance field). This mimics real biofilm: it
     grows toward open space, not into walls.
  4. growth is reset to 0.
  5. The player earns +100 points (score_colony).
  6. A signal is emitted → main.gd places the colony on the map.

What happens on the map (main.gd, lines 543–557):
  - The tile is changed to  GameData.Tile.BIOFILM
  - A new colony_cell node is spawned at that position.
  - A placement sound effect plays.
  - The camera shakes briefly (screen shake = 2 intensity, 0.15s).

The science behind it:
  In real microbiology, when a bacterium accumulates enough biomass
  it divides (binary fission). In CompLaB3D the threshold is
  B_max = 100 kg/m³. The game translates this into GR=100. The
  Cellular Automata model pushes excess biomass into neighboring
  pore cells, which is exactly what the "find best adjacent pore"
  logic does in the code.


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
 NOTE 3:  BIOFILM COLONY  —  Why we call them "SENTINELS"
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

 *** This is the key point your advisor asked about. ***

What is a sentinel?
  A sentinel is a guard who is posted at a fixed location and
  stays there permanently to watch over and protect an area.
  We use this word deliberately — here is why it fits:

The colony has THREE sentinel-like properties:

  1. PERMANENT  (it never leaves its post)
     Once placed, a colony exists forever. There is no timer,
     no lifespan, no decay. The code (colony_cell.gd) tracks
     age but never uses it to destroy the colony. The tile is
     permanently changed to BIOFILM. There is no function
     anywhere in the codebase that removes or destroys a colony.

  2. INDESTRUCTIBLE  (it cannot be killed)
     There is no damage system, no health bar, no way for rival
     microbes or any game event to destroy a colony. Rivals can
     walk THROUGH biofilm tiles (they are walkable), but they
     cannot remove them. The colony simply cannot die.

  3. PASSIVE AREA DENIAL  (it guards its zone by consuming resources)
     This is the most sentinel-like behavior. The code in main.gd
     (lines 485–498) runs every frame:

       for each colony on the map:
         for each substrate (food) on the map:
           if the food is within 1.5 tiles of the colony:
             there is a 2% chance PER FRAME to consume it

     At 60 frames per second, that 2% per frame means a substrate
     sitting 1.5 tiles from a colony has roughly:
       1 - (0.98)^60 ≈ 70% chance of being eaten within 1 second.

     The colony does not "attack." It does not move. It simply
     sits there and passively absorbs any food that appears
     nearby — starving out rival microbes in that area.

     This is EXACTLY what a sentinel does: it holds its position
     and controls the surrounding territory.

How this connects to real science:
  In real microbiology, established biofilms are:
    - Extremely difficult to remove (often 1000× more resistant
      to antibiotics than free-floating bacteria)
    - Permanently attached to surfaces
    - Dominant consumers of local nutrients — they create
      "nutrient depletion zones" around themselves where other
      organisms cannot compete

  The game's "sentinel" behavior directly models this:
    Permanent attachment  →  colony never moves or disappears
    Antibiotic resistance →  colony is indestructible
    Nutrient depletion    →  colony passively eats nearby substrates

  This is supported by the CompLaB3D research model, which uses
  Cellular Automata to simulate biofilm formation and nutrient
  competition in subsurface porous media.

Why sentinels matter for gameplay:
  - Each colony is worth +100 points.
  - Colonies deny food to rivals in a 1.5-tile radius.
  - Strategic placement creates a "defensive network" — if you
    place colonies near food-rich areas, rivals starve.
  - The level goal is to place enough colonies (typically 5).
  - This makes colony placement the CORE STRATEGY of the game:
    where you place your sentinels determines whether you win.

Summary (one sentence you can say to the audience):
  "We call them sentinels because, just like real biofilms, once
   they attach to a surface they are permanent, indestructible,
   and they dominate the local nutrient supply — effectively
   guarding that area of the map for the rest of the game."
