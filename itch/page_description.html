ARKE: Guardians of Earth

Play as a microbe. Save the planet.

I built a 3D biogeochemical reactive transport simulator (CompLaB3D) for my research at the University of Georgia. It models how microorganisms grow inside soil pores, consume substrates, form biofilms, and alter fluid flow — all at scales smaller than a grain of sand.

The problem? Explaining pore-scale biogeochemistry to non-scientists — or even to scientists in different fields — has always been surprisingly difficult. These processes are invisible to the naked eye and deeply abstract.

So I turned my research into a game.

What is ARKE?

You play as ARKE (from Greek arkhē = beginning/origin, the root of "Archaea"), a tiny methanotrophic archaeon living inside the pore spaces between soil and rock grains deep underground. Your mission: eat substrates, grow biomass, place colonies, and prevent greenhouse gases (methane CH4 and nitrous oxide N2O) from reaching the atmosphere.

Real Science, Real Gameplay

Everything in ARKE is based on real computational models from the CompLaB3D research framework:

  • Fluid flow — Water flows through pore channels driven by pressure gradients, simulated using the Lattice Boltzmann Method (LBM)
  • Microbial growth — Your growth follows Monod kinetics: μ = μmax × C / (Ks + C)
  • Biofilm expansion — Colonies spread using cellular automata, just like in the real simulator
  • Equilibrium chemistry — Chemical reactions (acid-base, carbonate speciation) solved with Newton-Raphson methods
  • Pore geometry — The game world is a real pore network extracted from simulated subsurface environments

Educational Use

ARKE was designed to also serve as an educational tool for:

  • High school & undergraduate courses in environmental science, hydrogeology, microbiology, and chemical engineering
  • Classroom demonstrations of subsurface biogeochemical processes
  • Science outreach — making the invisible visible and the abstract tangible

If you're an educator, feel free to use ARKE in your teaching. No permission needed.
